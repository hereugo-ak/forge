# SWOT → TOWS Matrix

## Purpose
SWOT is a **snapshot**, not a strategy. TOWS converts the SWOT snapshot into actionable strategic options by combining internal and external factors.

## Step 1: SWOT Analysis

### Strengths (Internal, Positive)
- [What does the company do well?]
- [What unique resources does it control?]
- [What do others see as strengths?]

### Weaknesses (Internal, Negative)
- [What can be improved?]
- [What should be avoided?]
- [What are others likely to see as weaknesses?]

### Opportunities (External, Positive)
- [What good opportunities face the company?]
- [What trends could benefit the company?]
- [What technology changes could help?]

### Threats (External, Negative)
- [What obstacles does the company face?]
- [What are competitors doing?]
- [What regulatory/tech changes could hurt?]

## Step 2: TOWS Matrix — Generate Strategic Options

### SO Strategies (Strengths + Opportunities)
Use strengths to capitalize on opportunities.
- [S1 + O1 → specific strategy]
- [S2 + O2 → specific strategy]

### WO Strategies (Weaknesses + Opportunities)
Overcome weaknesses to pursue opportunities.
- [W1 + O1 → specific strategy]
- [W2 + O2 → specific strategy]

### ST Strategies (Strengths + Threats)
Use strengths to avoid or mitigate threats.
- [S1 + T1 → specific strategy]
- [S2 + T2 → specific strategy]

### WT Strategies (Weaknesses + Threats)
Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats — defensive.
- [W1 + T1 → specific strategy]
- [W2 + T2 → specific strategy]

## HYPERION Application
The Strategy Analyst (Agent 14) always converts SWOT into TOWS. A generic strategist stops at SWOT. The HYPERION Strategy Analyst generates 3-5 concrete strategic options from the TOWS matrix and scores them on the Strategic Option Grid (feasibility, impact, risk, time to value, resource requirements).
